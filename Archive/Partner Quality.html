Background

A critical gap in partner
relationship management

As ServiceNow expanded its partner ecosystem, the Partner Digital Transformation team identified a gap in how partner-facing teams managed relationships, workflows, and performance metrics. Internal sales tools weren't built for the operational complexity of managing partnerships.

PRM Goal

Create a unified system that improves observability, predictability, and governance of the partner ecosystem — enabling earlier intervention and alignment around shared signals rather than subjective judgment.

My Role

Led end-to-end UX design for Partner Quality — from research synthesis and system framing to information architecture, interaction design, and prototyping. Collaborated with Product, Research, and Engineering to align on governance decisions and translate partner performance data into actionable signals.

Research Synthesis System Framing Information Architecture Interaction Design Prototyping

Design Constraints

The Partner Quality workflow had to balance system ambition with enterprise constraints:

  • Speed vs. platform performance
  • Real-time signal aggregation vs. API latency
  • Flexibility vs. design system governance
  • Cross-functional alignment vs. 8-week delivery pressure
Design Solutions

Four system-level shifts

Each solution addresses a specific failure mode identified through research — moving from passive monitoring to active, decision-relevant intelligence.

01

Portfolio-Level Decision Surface

Partner risk was visible but lacked lifecycle context, making it difficult for PMs to know when intervention mattered most. We reframed Partner Quality as a portfolio-level decision surface, organizing signals around pre-sale and post-sale stages and summarizing partner capability into a simple Experience State.

This shifted Partner Quality from a static dashboard to a situational awareness layer.

02

Decision-Relevant Driver Tags

Even with lifecycle framing, PMs still needed a faster way to understand why a partner was flagged. We introduced Partner Quality Driver Tags — plain-language labels that translate underlying signals into recognizable risk patterns.

Tags like "opportunity risk," "delivery gaps," and "experience limitations" provide multiple decision perspectives without adding UI complexity or alert fatigue.

03

Feature-Level Alerts → System-Level Decision Triggers

Alerts buried within Partner Quality often surfaced too late to influence key decisions. We reframed alerts as decision triggers and elevated them to PRM Home, the daily orientation layer for Partner Managers.

This shift surfaces critical signals before opportunity commitment or delivery execution — turning passive notifications into early indicators that shape cross-workflow decisions.

04

Consistent PRM Interaction Framework

Deep drill-downs and external filtering created latency, cognitive friction, and fragmented navigation. We introduced a consistent Orient → Diagnose → Act decision flow across all Level-1 sections.

Landing pages surface signals for prioritization. List views support fast investigation. Record views enable object-level actions.

Design Evolution

From dashboard to
decision support

Five iterations across 8 weeks — moving from data-heavy monitoring dashboards toward a focused, signal-driven decision layer. A four-stage discovery process that pivoted from dashboard-first assumptions toward first-principle system design.

01
Intent Alignment — Clarifying what value PRM needed to deliver

We began with a cross-functional workshop involving Partner Managers, Partner Success, and Operations stakeholders to align on what value PRM should provide and what unmet needs existed in partner management workflows. Conversations focused on lack of visibility into partner health, difficulty identifying experience deficits, and inconsistent signals across CRM, enablement, and operational tools.

02
Metric Discovery — Identifying how partner health was currently evaluated

We worked closely with the Partner Success team to identify the key metrics used to assess partner readiness and experience deficits, including implementation opportunity risk (ACV), deployment type and complexity, and partner experience maturity indicators (LAC%, CSAT, PLA). The working assumption was that consolidating these metrics clearly would enable Partner Managers to evaluate partner health more effectively.

03
Initial Design Direction — Dashboard-first thinking

With requirements and metrics defined, I moved into design exploration focused on clear visualization of partner health data, lifecycle-based grouping of metrics, and alerting patterns to surface risks early. This resulted in a dashboard-oriented solution emphasizing visibility, monitoring, and alerts. While the design reduced data fragmentation, early feedback revealed an unexpected outcome: Partner Managers experienced alert fatigue and continued to treat Partner Quality as "another dashboard" rather than a decision tool.

04
Four Pain Themes Surfaced from Usability Testing

Usability testing surfaced four recurring pain themes that went beyond surface-level UI issues. Navigation was difficult to discover, important signals required too much navigation to reach, it was hard to interpret why a partner was risky, and too many alerts reduced overall signal effectiveness. These insights reframed the problem: the issue wasn't lack of data visibility, but difficulty interpreting and acting on signals quickly.

05
From User Insights to System Design Decisions

Looking beyond interface issues, we reframed the problem using first-principle system thinking: Partner Quality should not function as a reporting dashboard, but as a decision-support surface that helps Partner Managers intervene at the right moment. Users struggled with navigation discoverability, excessive click depth, unclear filters, and missing alerts for opportunity movement — all symptoms of a system optimized for reporting rather than decision-making.

Design Reflection

From Features to System Intelligence

This project shifted my thinking from designing isolated features to designing systems that support decision-making under uncertainty. What began as a dashboard and visibility problem evolved into a platform-level challenge around signals, intervention timing, and cross-workflow coordination. Through usability testing and system mapping, I learned that enterprise value comes not from showing more data, but from helping people recognize what matters and act with confidence. This perspective now shapes how I approach agentic AI design: not as standalone conversational interfaces, but as decision-support coworkers embedded within operational systems—surfacing signals, synthesizing context, and supporting human judgment through continuous feedback loops.
